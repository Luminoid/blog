Focal Length

“Normal” Focal Length

  • Pupil Focal Length: 17mm
  • Telephoto - Normal - Wide Angle
  • For a “Full Frame” sensor, 36mm x 24mm (43.2mm diagonal), the “Normal” Focal Length lens is 50mm (43mm is mathematically normal)
  • Angle of View: 200mm(12deg), 70mm(34deg), 50mm(40deg), 35mm(60deg), 15mm (180deg), human eye(114deg)

Perspective effects

  • Normal focal length: normal rendering of space
  • Long focal length: compressing space
  • Short focal length: expanding space

Portrait en Creux (hollow portrait):

The person himself is not physically portrayed in such works. However, it’s the evidence of their persona, their personality, what makes them who they are in some significant manner that has a certain poignancy, a certain mystery but also a solid and tangible reality, that’s what becomes the subject of the picture.

Fixed or Zoom

Advantages of fixed focal length lens:

  • be more attuned to the physical space you’re in
  • wide aperture
  • a much less complicated device (sharper)

Special Lenses

Macro

Macro lenses are specially designed to permit focusing at very close distances to create what are called macro photographs.

Fisheye

For a camera with a full-frame sensor, a fisheye focal length would be anywhere from 5 millimeters to 16 millimeters.

Tilt-Shift

  • Shift: eliminate distortion through tilting the camera and creating effects of perspective; eliminate the reflection of the photographer from shiny surfaces
  • Tilt: adjust the plane of focus
  • Extreme tilt: extreme distortions of focus. (eg. LENSBABY)

Lens Accessories

Shades

  • Lens Hood
    • Limit the possibility that light will directly illuminate the front element of the lens, causing defraction and the effect that photographers call lens flare
    • Protect the lens
    • A longer focal length lens will have a longer lens hood because the angular view is so much narrower for that lens, and thus a narrower diameter hood can be used without blocking the edges of the image.
  • Lens Skirt: blocks environmental light from causing lens flare
  • Bellows Lens Shade: block light and insert filters

Filters

  • UV(ultraviolet) filter
  • polarizing filter
  • neutral density filter
  • close up filter (diopter lens)
  • starburst filter

Lens Adapters

  • Novoflex Lens Adapter

Depth of Field

Shallow / Broad

Determinants

  • Aperture
    • The more wide open the lens, the shallower the depth of field.
  • Focal Distance
    • The longer the lens, the shallower the depth of field.
  • Focal Length (The distance from the camera to the subject)
    • close subject, shallow depth of field
    • distant subject, broad depth of field

Time

Time as Fact

  • shutter speed (the time the lenses open)
  • aperture (the size of the opening)
  • ISO (the sensitivity of the sensors)
  • focal length (perspective and characteristic depth of field of the lens)
  • focal distance (the distance between the photographer and the subject)

Time as Fiction

  • slow and extremely slow shutter speeds
    • with the aperture fully stopped down to create crisp moments connected by blur
    • with aperture more wide open to create smooth, fluid blur within a moment, that has traces of movement or a change in it.
  • panning
    • light poles, light painting, camera tosses, kinetic photography
  • juxtapose

Principles of Photo Composition and Digital Image Post-Production

Course Site: https://www.coursera.org/learn/photo-composition

Elements of Design: Building Blocks of Composition

MAKING not TAKING pictures

A goal of composition is to create well-organized visual elements that give the viewer a path to follow through the picture, to help them understand the expression you intended.

To make photographs that will convey your responses to the special places, people and things that you encounter.

Shape: the 2-Dimensional Aspect

The more difference there is between the amount of light falling on the front of the object and the amount of light on the background, the easier it is to create silhouettes by changing the camera exposure.

Form: the 3-Dimensional Aspect

  • Looking at the relationship between light and shadow is one of the primary ways we emphasize form.
  • Occlusion: the overlapping of objects

Texture: the Untouched Aspect

  • Highlights and shadows are important to convey texture.
  • Contrasting textures: roughness and smoothness

Tone/Value: Shades of Gray

This aspect is primarily related to darkness and lightness in the scene itself, and in the ways the photographer can make the scene appear.

Zone System:
For each plus or minus number you adjust your exposure using the exposure value scale, also known as EV, you double or half the amount of light that will hit the censor.

Line: the Visible and Implied

Looking for lines that are both visible and invisible, real and implied, is a key activity for photographers who are keen on keeping their viewer’s eye moving.

  • Vertical line: solidity, strength
    • Vertical lines can also be used by the photographer to create a signal to the viewer that they do not want the viewer to go beyond the right edge, but to return back into the picture. By creating a barrier on the right side of the frame, the photographer gives the composition a sense of completion.
  • Horizontal line: direction, separation
  • Diagonal line: dynamic sense of movement
  • S-curved line: grace and smoothness
  • Radiant line: attractive and active

PATTERN: Recognition of Relationships

  • Repetition, similarity and direction.
  • Finding the vantage point is the key to success with pattern.
  • Barrel distortion

COLOR: Rainbow of Meanings

  • contrasting colors
  • monochromatic
  • color wheel: adjacent colors / opposite colors

Putting the Elements of Design together

Visual Weight: attraction and interest

Visual weight measures all of the different things that can cause someone to be interested in something.

The number one most attractive element in any picture will be the human beings who are a part of the composition.

Composition: Mapmaking and Bicycling

You should make pictures with a purpose. That purpose must not only be in terms of the subject, in other words, what the picture is about. But it must also be in terms of the map making that will guide the view to the visual elements through which you are conveying your content.

Shift the visual weight to create a balance.

Perspective: Drawing Us Through Space

Perspective is one of the organizational tools that photographers and other artists use to bring a sense of order and balance and direction to their compositions.

  • vanishing point
  • convergence

Symmetry

  • Balancing similar visual elements against one another
  • identical / similar without being identical

Asymmetry

  • Balancing interest, excitement, attraction
  • The rule of thirds: if one third of the picture area is devoted to a definable, singular thing or space and the remaining two-thirds to a different thing or space, they will generally be well balanced.

    • Luminance: color brightness

Photography Techniques: Light, Content, and Sharing

Course Site: https://www.coursera.org/learn/photography-techniques

Content

The Portrait: document and image

Portrait is an image that somehow gets at something unique or distinctive about the subject.

  • Lighting: Soft light is much more common in all kinds of portraits
  • Focal length: Longer focal lengths are generally preferable
  • Focal point: Focusing on the eyes is a widely accepted practice
  • Framing:
    • Head Shot (could include upper shoulders and neck to top of head)
    • Half-Length Shot (from the waist up)
    • Three-Quarter-Length Shot (from the knee up)
    • Full Length Shot (full body)
  • Background: Simple backgrounds are the easiest and sometimes most effective. Watch out for undesirable connections, poles sticking out of people’s heads or wires coming out, that look like they’re coming from the ears.
  • Creative, expressive and experimental approaches:
    • high key lighting
    • multiple exposure or blur
    • staged tableau or a deliberately glitched or manipulated image

The Landscape: document and image

Landscape is an image that conveys something distinctive or striking about the landforms with or without people.

The Still Life: document and image

The task often involves not just the choice of objects and how to arrange them spatially, but also how to put them into relationships that make them fascinating even though they might not be all that fascinating by themselves.

  • shooting from different angles

The Tableau: fictions

Since the invention of photography, this genre has had a persistent popularity, not only for recreating old paintings, but also for creating new narrative scenes in the tableau style as a posed photograph.

Manipulated Landscape: fictions

Manipulated Landscape involves manipulating traditional or conventional kinds of landscape, and it is in a variety of ways which interfere with their character as a document, and which pushed them in the direction of a visual fiction.

Ambient Light

Light As Energy

Three things that can happen when light moves from a source:

  • Reflection: Light hits the surface and bounces off. (specular and diffuse)
  • Absorption: Light hits a surface and it’s absorbed, converted to heat.
  • Transmission: Light hits a surface and passes through.

Light As an Element of Photography

  • Direction: where light comes from
  • Quality: whether the light is soft or hard
  • Intensity: how bright, dim or dark the light is
  • Color: both temperature and reflected and absorbed color

Colors

Color is a product of source and reflection.

There are both reflected and diffused light, in the environment that will be picked up by your subject and your camera.

Tips and Techniques: Ambient Light and People

  • When to shoot
    • The golden hour: the hour before sunset, and the hour after sunrise
    • Twilight: when the sun is just below the horizon in the morning and the evening
    • The blue hour: when the sun is very low, below the horizon, even though it’s not yet fully dark
  • A fill flash is not used to get an acceptable level of exposure but rather to minimize or reduce shadows in the scene.
  • The larger the light in relation to the size of the subject, the softer will be the light.

Tips and Techniques: Ambient Light and Landscapes

  • The time of day (where the sun is in the sky)
  • Position
  • Foreground

Tips And Techniques: Ambient Light and Things

  • The introduction of small portable lights, or the the light of an external flash, or a speed light, can make a huge difference.

Controlled Light

Basic Principles

  • Normal vs Abnormal
  • Hard/Direct vs Soft/Diffuse
  • Inverse Square Law: $\text{intensity}\propto{1\over{\text{distance}^2}}$

Camera Flash Types and Functions

  • flash mode
    • manual
    • automatic
    • E-TTL(Evaluative-Through The Lens): a meter reading of the ambient and strobe light is made by the camera, which sends a signal to the flash, to emit just enough light to fill in the shadows and supplement the exposure based on the continuous light already in the scene.
    • Multi mode (repeating flash): stroboscopic effect
    • Slow sync: the camera made the correct exposure for the flash on the foreground and it selected a long enough shutter speed to get sufficient exposure for the details in the background.
  • balance natural light and flash light
  • red eye control
  • ${\text{Guide # }\over{\text{Subject Distance (feet)}}} = \text{f.stop}$
  • Kelvin Temperature
    • Sun: 5500
    • Flash: 5500
    • Tungsten bulb: 3200
    • Candle: 1900
  • auxiliary flash (shoe mount flash): dedicated / non-dedicated

Flash and the Focal Plane Shutter

  • Sync Speed: the fastest shutter speed for the whole image frame to be fully exposed by the flash light
  • Front curtain flash: the flash is triggered at the first possible moment after the curtains have completely uncovered the sensor.
  • Rear curtain flash: the flash is triggered at the last possible moment before the curtains have begun to cover up the sensor. (longer shutter speed)

Flash Tools

  • Capability to tilt upwards and twist from side to side.
  • White plastic card (eliminating raccoon animal effect)
  • Diffuser
  • Flash bracket

Kitchen Table Studio: Lighting an Object

  • Accessories
    • shooting table (table + middle gray background paper + gaffers tape)
    • tripod
    • lamp
    • reflector
  • Establishing a “figure-ground” relationship through light and shadow is important in conveying the position of a 3-dimensional object in space
  • The 3-dimensionality of the space behind an object can be indicated by creating a graduated shadowing of the background with a large sheet of foam-core or other board in between the lamp and the background

ICC Profiles and Color Management

In color management, an ICC profile is a set of data that characterizes a color input or output device, or a color space, according to standards promulgated by the International Color Consortium (ICC).

Calibrating for consistency: theory, monitor and printer

  • Viewing works under light that has a white balance of 5,000 degrees Kelvin has long been an industry standard for printers and professionally managed art galleries too.

The Critique

When a photographer creates a picture, the following steps are taken:

  • determine the subject (whether it is visible or an invisible concept)
  • determine the photographic techniques that matters most
  • determine the important visual elements
  • determine the composition of those visual elements

When we critique, we may begin by following the path of the photographer, in the same order:

  • name the subject as we see it
  • describe the techniques that we think were used, and whether they were used well
  • describe the important visual elements, and evaluate the composition/organization
  • decide whether the photographer was successful or not, in whole or in part, in communicating to the average viewer what the subject is
